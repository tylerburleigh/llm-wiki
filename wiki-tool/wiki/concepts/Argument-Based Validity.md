> [!tldr]
> Kane's argument-based approach to test validation treats the interpretation of a score as a chain of inferences (explanation, evaluation, generalization, extrapolation, utilization) and evaluates the test by the evidence supporting each link; Williamson-Xi-Breyer map the automated-scoring evaluation framework onto this chain.

## Definition

> [!source] Argument-based validity comes from Kane (1992, 2006).
> The paper cites Kane's "argument-based approach to test validation (Kane, 1992, 2006)" as the conceptual backbone for combining different aspects of automated-scoring validity "in an integrated fashion." [[Williamson Xi Breyer 2012 - Framework for Automated Scoring]]

## The five inferences and their mapping

> [!source] Each of the framework's five emphasis areas corresponds to a Kane inference.
> "These areas of emphasis correspond respectively to the explanation, evaluation, generalization, extrapolation, and utilization inferences in Kane's argument-based approach to test validation (Kane, 2006)." The alignment, as stated in the paper's Table 1, is:
> - Construct relevance and representation -> Explanation
> - Association with human scores -> Evaluation
> - Generalizability of scores -> Generalization
> - Association with independent measures -> Extrapolation
> - Impact on decisions and consequences -> Utilization
>
> [[Williamson Xi Breyer 2012 - Framework for Automated Scoring]]

## Key Claims

> [!source] Earlier automated-scoring work laid out the mapping incrementally.
> Clauser, Kane, and Swanson (2002) "made the initial attempt to unfold validity issues regarding automated scoring in the larger context of a validity argument for the assessment." Xi et al. (2008) "applied a more elaborated framework for evaluating the use of automated scoring for a speaking practice test" and explicated unique validity issues per inference. Enright and Quinlan (2010) used Kane's framework for e-rater on TOEFL iBT Writing. [[Williamson Xi Breyer 2012 - Framework for Automated Scoring]]

> [!source] Fairness is an aspect of the validity argument, not a separate criterion.
> The paper adopts Xi's definition of fairness as "comparable validity for identifiable and relevant groups" (Xi, 2010b) and treats subgroup analyses as extensions of the same inference chain. [[Williamson Xi Breyer 2012 - Framework for Automated Scoring]]

> [!analysis] Kane's chain provides a why for each framework area, not just a what.
> Explanation asks whether the score means what the construct says it means; evaluation asks whether the scoring process produced the score accurately; generalization asks whether the score transfers across tasks and forms; extrapolation asks whether the score predicts behavior beyond the test; utilization asks whether the decision made from the score is sound. A framework area not tied to an inference would be decorative; one tied to two inferences would be overloaded.

> [!analysis] The mapping is not strict one-to-one in practice.
> Association with independent measures is mapped to extrapolation, but within-test correlations are closer to evaluation/generalization in spirit. Treating "independent measures" broadly lets one area absorb multiple inference flavors; this is convenient but blurs the formal argument. Readers tracing a specific validity challenge should expect to revisit which inference is at stake.
